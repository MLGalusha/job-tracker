# Story

Mason's narrative — where he came from, how he got here, what he wants. Drafting skills (resume-tailor, cover-letter-writer) should read this for tone, momentum, and the threads worth pulling on.

## Origin and shape

Grew up in Kenna, West Virginia, on his Nana's property. Main house, barn, and a one-story house up the hill where he lived. Childhood was exploration-heavy — time in the woods, building shelters and fires, carrying small trees around for makeshift structures, pulling clay from creeks. That place still matters to him; he's talked about wanting to own it one day.

Growing up he leaned more explorer than builder, with an eye for creating cool things. As an adult the balance has flipped — he leans more builder now, but the explorer instinct is still underneath everything and it shows up in how he picks problems (follow curiosity, not a roadmap) and how he works (build to find out, not research to avoid finding out).

Married. His wife Bailey loves art and drawing; their anniversary is August 11, 2023. He's reserved by nature and has historically not enjoyed social media — his "build in silence" instinct is real, not a pose. At one point he deleted his GitHub profile README because public-facing profile stuff felt like the wrong mode. This matters for how outreach gets written: self-promotion is not native, and drafts that sound like it will feel wrong to him.

## One-paragraph version (resume-ready)

Self-taught engineer who pivoted into AI-native software development after an early fascination with ChatGPT and generative tools in 2023. Completed UNC Chapel Hill's 6-month AI bootcamp in late 2024, where the capstone was training a real-time sheet-music generation model on GCP. Spent 2025 deliberately coding by hand to learn the fundamentals, shipping SonicGen (a 14,000 line Shazam-style fingerprinting engine, zero agents) and building the instincts needed to evaluate AI-generated code. When VTR was proposed in late 2025, switched to agent-directed development and the transition was immediate because he already understood the code agents produce. In early 2026 quit a full-time cooking job to go all-in on engineering, and shipped _Voices That Remain_ (voicesthatremain.com), a production full-stack digital archive for historical personal letters (with an AI pipeline inside it for transcription and metadata extraction), in roughly three weeks of focused work.

## Why AI-native, why agents

He's been a power AI user since 2023, before the market caught up. Coding _feels_ like a game to him with agents, and one person can now build things that used to require a team. But the important part is the sequence: he spent all of 2025 coding by hand because he was worried that skipping fundamentals would mean he'd never actually learn to code and would be unable to get a job. SonicGen (14k LOC, zero agents) is the proof that he can write complex software without AI assistance. Only after building that foundation did he switch to agents for VTR, and the transition was trivially easy because he already understood everything the agents were producing. He wasn't learning to code from agents; he was using agents to accelerate code he already knew how to write. Any drafting skill should treat this as a _capability built on a foundation_, not a crutch. The strongest framing: "I coded by hand until I was confident I understood the code, then I started directing agents and it was an easy transition because I already knew what good output looked like."

The long-term picture isn't luxury. It's land, a workshop full of tools, country living, dark skies, and the capacity to build in the physical world alongside the digital one. Teaching future kids to engineer and build things. Entrepreneurship is the aspiration — not retirement into passivity, but working from freer environments on his own terms.

He frames money as leverage for the people he cares about, not as personal indulgence. This shows up in cover letter tone: his "why" for wanting a real engineering role is both ambition and protection, and both are authentic.

Core values he's named directly: self-improvement, unlocking potential, real family connection without fake fronts, making other people's lives better. Realness is load-bearing — he reacts badly to anything that feels performative or overpolished, which is the same instinct that produced voice.md.

## Learning style

He learns by building, not by tutorials or books. Broad research-first approaches backfire on him — he rabbit-holes, overwhelms, and stops. The pattern that works: implement first, hit a real problem, ask one narrow focused question, solve that, zoom back out. He described this to me as a "micro-question" approach. This is relevant for interview prep (study-guide work should break into small concrete tasks, not survey topics) and for how he evaluates engineering cultures (he prefers environments where you ship to learn, not study to ship).

## Operator instinct, not just builder instinct

At Alamo, he wasn't just cooking. He was paying attention to back-of-house systems, scheduling failures, communication gaps, leadership breakdowns, and operational inefficiencies — analyzing the operation like a builder and operator would. Staffclaw and OpenClaw grew directly out of that observation habit. This is a real thread: he's wired to notice the system around whatever he's doing, not just the task in front of him. It's a founding-engineer trait and worth pulling on for early-stage roles.
