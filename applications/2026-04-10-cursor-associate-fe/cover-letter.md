# Cover Letter -- Cursor, Associate Field Engineer

I use Cursor every day to build production systems. Not as a side experiment, as my primary development method. I have shipped 58,700 lines of production TypeScript through it. That gives me something most applicants cannot claim: I know the product from the inside, including what it does well, where it struggles, and what makes the difference between a productive agent session and a frustrating one.

The project I built most of that code for is Voices That Remain, a digital archive with a 4-stage AI pipeline using GPT-5.4 with structured outputs, tool calling, and vision. It is deployed on GCP and live at voicesthatremain.com. I designed human-in-the-loop review workflows into the pipeline because the end users are not engineers. They need clear interfaces and the ability to validate what the AI produced before it reaches production. That design instinct, building for people who are not you, is the core of what a field engineer does when helping customers adopt a new tool.

Before Cursor, I wrote SonicGen: 14,000 lines of Python, by hand, with zero agent assistance. A Shazam-style audio fingerprinting engine from scratch. I did this deliberately to build the kind of deep code fluency that makes agent-directed development productive instead of dangerous. When I help a customer adopt Cursor, I will not be reciting documentation. I will be drawing on real experience with the transition from manual coding to agent-directed development, including the specific habits and workflows that make it work.

I also built Staffclaw by reverse-engineering 92 API endpoints from an undocumented scheduling platform. I mapped the system, understood the business logic, and built a full stack replacement. That is the kind of diagnostic thinking a field engineer needs when dropped into a customer's environment where the documentation is thin and the systems are complex.
